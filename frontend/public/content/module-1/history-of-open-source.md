## A Short History: How Open Source Came to Be

You might think "sharing code freely" is a new thing, but the story actually starts way back in the early days of computers. Let me tell you a story...

---

## 🕰️ Act 1: The Age of Sharing (1950s-1970s)

In the beginning, software was just something that came with the computer hardware. If you bought a computer, you got the code with it. Developers freely shared their programs with each other — it was like a big community potluck where everyone brought a dish and everyone ate.

There was no such thing as "proprietary software." Code was just... code.

---

## 🔒 Act 2: The Gates Close (1970s-1980s)

Then something changed. Companies realized they could make money by **hiding** the code and only selling the compiled version (the one humans can't read). A famous open letter from a young Bill Gates in 1976 complained that hobbyists were sharing software without paying for it.

The era of "proprietary software" had begun. Companies locked their code away, and if you wanted to use it, you had to agree to strict rules — and you could never see how it worked.

---

## 🦸 Act 3: The Rebel (1983)

Enter **Richard Stallman**. He was a brilliant programmer at MIT who got frustrated when he couldn't fix a broken printer because the manufacturer had locked the code.

He decided: **enough is enough.**

Stallman launched the **GNU Project** in 1983, with the goal of creating a completely free operating system. He believed software should respect four essential freedoms:

1. Run it for any purpose
2. Study how it works (need the source code for this)
3. Redistribute copies to help others
4. Improve it and share your improvements

He created the **Free Software Foundation** and a special license called the **GPL** (General Public License) that ensured code would stay free forever.

---

## 🐧 Act 4: The Penguin That Changed Everything (1991)

In 1991, a Finnish student named **Linus Torvalds** posted a message online:

> "I'm doing a (free) operating system (just a hobby, won't be big and professional like GNU)..."

That "hobby" became the **Linux kernel** — the heart of what we now call Linux. Thousands of developers around the world started contributing. Today, Linux runs:

- 90%+ of the world's cloud servers
- All Android phones
- Most supercomputers
- Your Wi-Fi router, your TV, your car

---

## 🤝 Act 5: "Open Source" Gets a Name (1998)

Some people loved the idea of sharing code but felt the term "Free Software" was confusing (it's about freedom, not price). In 1998, a group of developers coined the term **"Open Source"** to focus on the practical benefits of collaboration rather than the philosophical arguments.

This was a turning point. Big companies like Netscape, and later IBM, Google, and Microsoft, started embracing open source. Today, even companies that were once fiercely proprietary now have massive open source projects.

---

## 🌍 Where We Are Today

Open source is no longer a niche hobby. It's the foundation of modern technology:

- **Linux** powers the internet
- **Python, JavaScript, Go, Rust** — all open source languages
- **VS Code, Firefox, WordPress** — tools billions use daily
- **AI frameworks** like PyTorch and TensorFlow are open source

And the best part? **You're now part of this story.**

---

## 🧠 The Key Takeaway

Open source isn't just about code — it's about a belief that **when we work together openly, we build better things than when we work alone in secret**.

Every person who contributes, even a tiny fix or a documentation typo, is continuing a story that started decades ago. And now it's your turn to add your chapter.
