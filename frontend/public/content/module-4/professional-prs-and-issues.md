## Being a good open source contributor isn't just about writing good code — it's about being a professional and considerate collaborator.

Think of it like **being a guest at a potluck dinner**. You wouldn't show up empty-handed, eat all the food without talking to anyone, or bring a dish that nobody asked for. The same courtesies apply to pull requests and issues.

---

### 🚫 Avoid These Common Mistakes

**1. The Hit-and-Run PR**

Creating a Pull Request and then disappearing when reviewers ask questions or request changes.

> **Instead**: Stick around! Respond to comments, explain your choices, and make the requested updates. A PR is a conversation, not a delivery.

**2. Spam Pull Requests**

Making tiny, meaningless edits — like adding a single space or fixing one comma — just to boost your GitHub contribution count.

This is especially common during events like Hacktoberfest. But maintainers can spot these from a mile away, and they'll mark them as **spam**.

> **Instead**: Make contributions that actually help. Even fixing a typo is fine if it genuinely improves the project — but don't go hunting for trivial changes just for the sake of it.

**3. Working Without Assignment**

Jumping into a complex issue without checking if someone else is already working on it.

> **Instead**: Comment on the issue saying "I'd like to work on this" and wait for a maintainer to assign it to you. This prevents two people from doing the same work.

**4. Duplicate Issues**

Opening a bug report without searching existing issues first.

> **Instead**: Before creating a new issue, search for keywords related to your problem. Someone may have already reported it — and maybe there's even a fix in progress!

---

> [!CAUTION]
> Spamming projects or ignoring contribution guidelines can get your PR tagged as spam, which might lead to your GitHub account being restricted. Always make meaningful, thoughtful contributions.

---

### 🧠 Key Takeaway

Be a considerate contributor. Respond to feedback, make meaningful changes, check for existing work, and search before reporting. A little professionalism goes a long way in open source.
